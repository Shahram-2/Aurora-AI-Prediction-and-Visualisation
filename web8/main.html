NASA Science · Heliophysics

What Are Auroras?

Nature's most spectacular light show — a visible record of the Sun's energy colliding with Earth's magnetic shield, painted across the polar sky.

By the numbers
100km
Minimum altitude at which auroras form
400–800
km/s — solar wind speed reaching Earth
1–3
Days for a CME to travel from Sun to Earth
11yr
Solar cycle — aurora activity peaks at solar max
How auroras form
Step 01

The Sun Erupts

The Sun constantly emits a stream of charged particles called the solar wind. During intense events — solar flares or coronal mass ejections (CMEs) — it hurls billions of tonnes of magnetised plasma into space at up to 3,000 km/s.

Heliophysics
Step 02

150 Million Kilometres

The solar wind crosses the vast distance between the Sun and Earth in 1–3 days. NASA's ACE and DSCOVR satellites sit at the L1 Lagrange point, providing roughly 15–60 minutes of advance warning before the particles reach us.

Solar Wind · 1 AU
Step 03

Magnetic Reconnection

Earth's magnetic field normally deflects solar particles. But when the IMF's Bz component turns southward, it reconnects with Earth's northward field — tearing open a gateway. Particles then pour down the field lines toward the polar caps.

Magnetosphere · Bz
Step 04

Atoms Light Up

Energised particles collide with oxygen and nitrogen atoms 80–300 km above Earth. The atoms absorb energy, jump to an excited state, then release it as photons of light. The altitude and gas type determine the colour — green from oxygen at 100 km, red higher up, blue from nitrogen at the base.

Photon Emission · Atmosphere
The colour palette

Each aurora colour is a signature of a specific gas at a specific altitude. Like a chemical fingerprint — written in light across the sky.
